OpenHunting.io

Threat Intelligence
Visit Platform ↗

OpenHunting.io is an open-source initiative dedicated to making threat hunting intelligence information and tools accessible to everyone in the cybersecurity community. Built around the philosophy that "tools are just tools, you are the genius behind them," it offers a massive repository of structured threat intel.

Threat Library

A continuously updated collection of threat information. The platform automatically aggregates and structures intel to give security researchers instant access to widespread IOCs (Indicators of Compromise) and attack behaviors.

Threat Reports

Detailed investigation write-ups regarding the latest threats. These reports break down attack chains, malware behavior, and threat actor methodologies, serving as an excellent resource for learning real-world analysis.

Hunting Tools

A suite of specialized utilities designed to aid the threat hunting process. This includes tools for footprinting, WHOIS enumeration, and proactive scanning against newly discovered vulnerabilities.

Learning & Experimentation Guide

Phase 1: Information Gathering

Start your workflow by checking the Threat Report section. Identify a recently reported vulnerability (e.g., a new CVE). Read the report to understand the "how" and "why" of the exploit. Then, head to the Threat Library to find associated IP addresses, domains, and hashes linked to the threat actors actively exploiting it.

Phase 2: Utilizing the Tools

Leverage the Threat Hunting Tools and Whois modules provided on the platform. Plug in the malicious infrastructure assets you found in Phase 1. Practice enumerating and mapping out the attacker's network footprint. Cross-reference the data within your own terminal environments or SOC tools.

Phase 3: Defensive Engineering

The ultimate goal of OpenHunting is turning intelligence into defense. Take the IOCs and TTPs (Tactics, Techniques, and Procedures) you discovered and write Sigma rules, YARA rules, or Snort signatures to detect these threats in a lab environment. Try executing safe simulations of the attack to verify if your rules work.
